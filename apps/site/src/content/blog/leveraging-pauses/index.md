> Draft.

There's a particular gap in the day that no one plans for: you've just finished something, and you haven't quite started the next thing. Thirty seconds, maybe two minutes. It almost always gets spent the same way — a reflexive reach for a feed, and then the gap is gone and so is a bit of the momentum.

That pause is not dead time. It's the most useful moment you'll get all day, and it's already there for free.

### The pause is a natural cue

Habits need a trigger, and the hardest part of journaling is that nothing reminds you to do it. But finishing a task _is_ a reminder — a clean, frequent, unmissable one. You don't have to schedule the log. You just attach it to a thing that already happens five, ten, fifteen times a day.

### Punctuation for the day

Without pauses, work runs together into one long undifferentiated blur. A line in the gap is punctuation — it ends a sentence. _"Shipped the export fix."_ Full stop. The act of writing it is also the act of actually closing the task instead of letting it bleed into the next one.

### A better thing to do with the gap

The reach for the feed is filling the pause with someone else's day. Writing a line fills it with your own. Same thirty seconds; the difference is whether, at 5pm, the day belongs to you or to the scroll.
