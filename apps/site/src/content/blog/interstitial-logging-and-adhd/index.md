> Draft. Not medical advice — one person's experience and a habit, not a treatment.

Two things make a workday slippery for a lot of people with ADHD: **time blindness** (an hour and ten minutes feel the same from the inside) and a **working memory that drops what it was holding** the moment something interrupts it. You look up and it's 4pm and you genuinely cannot account for the afternoon.

Interstitial logging pushes against both, and it does it without asking your brain to do the thing it's bad at.

### Timestamps externalize time

You don't have to _estimate_ how long the billing bug took if the log already says `9:02` start and `9:48` done. The clock did the counting. Over a week, the log quietly teaches you your own pace — which is usually nothing like the pace you assumed.

### The line is a catch for working memory

The dangerous moment is the transition: you finish something, your attention scatters, and the half-formed next thought evaporates. Writing one line at that moment — _"fixed it, it was a timezone off-by-one, next: onboarding empty state"_ — moves the thought out of your head and onto disk before it can leak. You're not trusting memory; you're not trusting it on purpose.

### Why it survives when other systems don't

Most ADHD productivity systems die because they demand maintenance: tags, projects, a daily review you'll skip by Thursday. Interstitial logging has exactly one move — write a line when you switch — and the timestamp does the organizing for free. There's no streak to break and nothing to "keep up."
