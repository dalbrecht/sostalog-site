> Draft.

Almost everyone has started a journal. Almost no one is still keeping the one they started. The usual explanation is discipline, but that's not it. The thing that kills a journaling habit is **friction** — the small tax you pay every single time before you can write a word.

It's death by a thousand tiny frictions:

- Open the app. Wait for it to sync.
- Sign in again because the session expired.
- Which notebook? Which folder? Which tag?
- Pick a template. Set the mood. Choose a font.
- _Now_ write — except the thought you had thirty seconds ago is gone.

Each step is trivial on its own. Together they're a wall, and the wall is highest exactly when the urge to write is smallest — which is most of the time.

### Friction is where habits go to die

A habit is a loop: cue, action, reward. Friction lengthens the action until the loop stops closing. You don't decide to quit journaling. You just, one busy day at a time, stop paying the tax.

### Designing it out

The only durable fix is to make the cost of writing approach zero:

- **No account.** Nothing to sign into, nothing to expire.
- **Local-first.** It opens instantly because it isn't waiting on a server.
- **One input.** No folder, no template, no decision. Cursor's already there.
- **No formatting to fuss over.** Plain text and a little markdown, nothing to style.

The goal is that the gap between _I want to write this down_ and _it's written down_ is too small to talk yourself out of.
